2026 TSDL Season Preview: Arizona Diamondbacks

By Jeff Passan — 2026-02-26 — 12 min read

Introduction

The Impossible Champions

There's a version of this story that ends with Cote Nichols' Arizona Diamondbacks as a cautionary tale — a dynasty that detonated out of the gate, racked up eight awards in its inaugural season, and then spent the next two years quietly bleeding out while the rest of the league caught up. In 2024, they finished 7-12. Sixteenth overall. Their consolation bracket finish of 17th was polite only by the grace of actually showing up. The floor had arrived, and it creaked.

And then 2025 happened.

The Diamondbacks limped into the postseason as the eighth and final seed. They had just enough wins to make it — 11 of them, to be exact — and just enough to be underestimated by everyone who had watched them tread water for two years. They beat the Atlanta Braves, the league's regular-season juggernaut with a 16-3 record, in the divisional round. They beat the Las Vegas Athletics, the team that had edged them by a single half-point in the regular season, in a bruising two-week conference series. Then they dispatched the St. Louis Cardinals to claim the championship. An 8-seed. Three wins over the top-four seeds. The most improbable title in TSDL history.

This is not a rebuilding team. This is not a team coasting on a fluke postseason run. This is a roster built on some of the best-value contracts in the history of the league, anchored by young stars who are getting better, and fortified by a minor league system that could send shockwaves through the active roster before the season even begins. The 2026 Arizona Diamondbacks don't just have a puncher's chance. They have a case to be considered the most dangerous team in the league.

Offseason Review

In-Season Moves: Building a Champion

Every championship team has a story about the decisions that made it possible. For Arizona, it begins in the spring before the 2025 season, with a MiLB draft that added the foundation for sustained contention.

MiLB Draft: Armed with the 9th pick in each round — a comfortable mid-lottery position — Nichols invested aggressively in upside. The headliner was Nick Kurtz, a 22-year-old first baseman scooped up with the 9th overall selection. Kurtz wasn't just a prospect stash; he was a player who would go on to produce 428.5 fantasy points across 117 games in 2025, proving he belonged in any lineup in the league. Round 2 brought Braden Montgomery (overall pick 27), a 22-year-old outfielder whose prospect profile generated debate — one major scouting system has him as a top-20 player in baseball, while another is more measured in the high-60s. That divergence makes Montgomery one of the more volatile but intriguing profiles in the system. Rounds 3 through 5 added Emiliano Teodo, Luke Keaschall, and Alex Freeland as deeper lottery tickets and organizational depth. Retained from the prior year without using draft capital: Luis Morales, Kyle Manzardo, Samuel Basallo, Ben Joyce, and Robby Snelling — five established names who entered 2025 as organizational cornerstones. The MiLB draft grade was quietly excellent. Five new picks, five retained keepers, and a first-round selection who would immediately contribute at the major league level. A−

FA Auction ($51 spent): With a strong returning roster, Nichols spent conservatively at the pre-season auction — and it looked like exactly the right call. The singular win was Spencer Torkelson at $8, a 23-year-old first baseman/third baseman who delivered 416.5 fantasy points in 155 games. That contract now sits at $9 heading into 2026, making it one of the cleanest auction values of any team in the league. The team also took a $21 swing on Max Scherzer, a veteran move that didn't translate to a 2026 roster spot, and added Jhonkensy Noel ($10) and Tyler Fitzgerald ($9), neither of whom made the cut for this year. Spending only $51 with surplus auction capital looked underutilized in the moment. In retrospect, it signaled a team that didn't need to overhaul — it needed depth and got it in Torkelson.

In-Season Trades & Pickups: The biggest in-season move came via a April trade with the Philadelphia Phillies. Arizona sent Jordan Romano and Josh Jung ($5, Year 3) to Philadelphia and received Alex Call and Devin Williams ($12, Year 4). Williams, who ended up in the confirmed free agent pool for 2026, was a championship-run rental: a high-leverage reliever who contributed 300.5 points on the season and provided postseason value before Arizona chose not to carry him into 2026. The trade looked unimpressive on paper but delivered exactly what a championship contender needs — bullpen reinforcement at a critical juncture. Arizona also churned the waiver wire aggressively, spending $79 in FAAB across 43 pickups. The standout was Trevor Megill, a reliever claimed in mid-April who went on to produce 342 fantasy points in 50 games. Megill parlayed that production into a $17 keeper contract for 2026.

Season Analysis

2025 Season in Review

The Diamondbacks' regular season defied easy categorization. They entered the year as quiet contenders, burst out of the gate 4-0 and held the top power rank through the first four weeks, then gradually settled into the middle of the standings as the schedule's variance sorted itself out. From Week 7 onward, they barely moved off the eighth seed line. Not an elite team. Not a bottom-dweller. A solid roster with championship-quality personnel and a 19-game schedule that didn't hand them enough comfortable matchups to climb higher.

The numbers tell the story of a genuinely strong team. Arizona's 7,986.5 total fantasy points ranked first in the league, surpassing the average by nearly 1,300 points. Their hitting-to-pitching split was a near-perfect 50.9/49.1 — a balance that suggests roster construction by design rather than luck. They weren't dependent on a loaded lineup masking a weak rotation, or vice versa. They were complete.

The regular season's ugliest stretch ran from Week 5 through Week 7, when Arizona dropped three straight and posted a low of just 213 points against Houston in Week 6. The team's biggest wins were decisive — a 144-point margin over Chicago in Week 10, a 139-point shellacking of New York in Week 14 — but their losses could be equally brutal, including a 135-point beatdown by Atlanta in Week 5. Inconsistency is the right word. But their floor was competitive enough to stay in the playoff picture, and that was all that mattered.

Then came the postseason.

The Diamondbacks drew the Atlanta Braves — the 16-3 regular season champion, the team that had blown them out by 135 points just three months earlier — in the divisional round. Arizona's response was to outscore them 749.5 to 563 across two weeks. It wasn't an upset. It was a statement. The conference round against Las Vegas came down to 34 points over two weeks, a margin so thin that one rough week could have erased it entirely. Arizona held. The championship against the Cardinals was decided by 41.5 points — a comfortable margin only in the context of what had just come before it.

The 2025 Arizona Diamondbacks went into the postseason as the eighth seed and left as the only team that matters. In TSDL lore, that run belongs in its own chapter.

The best midseason addition was Trevor Megill, claimed off waivers in mid-April for minimal cost. Megill carved out a substantial bullpen role and justified his retained contract heading into 2026. His acquisition — at essentially no cost — exemplified the kind of roster management that separates championship rosters from the rest of the field.

Roster Moves

Offseason Roster Cuts

Roster surgery this offseason was minimal — a reflection of a team that built correctly and has few obvious mistakes to correct. All three cuts were first-year contracts at the minimum $1 salary: clean, painless, and strategically irrelevant to the championship core.

Chase Meidroth — $1, Year 1 | 260.0 FPts: A serviceable contributor who simply didn't project well enough to hold a roster spot over more established options. At $1 with no escalation cost, this is a routine trim. Meidroth heads to the free agent pool as a depth piece for teams with auction budget to burn.

Yoan Moncada — $1, Year 1 | 186.5 FPts: The veteran third baseman didn't provide the production necessary to earn a spot on a roster this deep, particularly at a position covered by Junior Caminero. An obvious cut with zero long-term cost.

Matt Brash — $1, Year 1 | 190.5 FPts: A pitcher who contributed modestly but offered insufficient reliability to compete with the quality arms already on the staff. The decision to release him was straightforward. Arizona's rotation doesn't need reclamation projects at this stage.

Three cuts, $3 freed, zero major decisions deferred. The champion's offseason roster housekeeping was as efficient as the rest of their operation.

The Roster

The Core — Returning Players

Twenty players. $226 in keeper salary. Thirty-four dollars left for the FA auction. Those numbers will define Arizona's 2026 ceiling as much as any individual performance. The keeper salary chart tells the story of a roster that has accumulated genuine stars at bargain prices alongside a growing number of expensive veterans whose best production windows may be narrowing. It's a team built on excellent value contracts at the top — and it shows.

Let's start with what no amount of money can buy: two of the three best value contracts in the league, both on the same roster.

Paul Skenes — SP
SP | 2026 Salary: $5 | Contract Year 3 | 2025: 526 FPts, 16.4 FPts/G

There is no better contract in The Sandlot Dynasty League. At $5 in Year 3 — escalating to $10 next season — Skenes is a top-three dynasty pitcher in baseball at a salary that belongs on a waiver-wire special. He made 32 starts in 2025 and produced 526 fantasy points. His per-game average of 16.4 is the kind of number that makes fantasy analysts squint at their screens. Arizona gets one more year of this obscene value in 2026 before the cost creep begins in earnest. The window on this discount is narrowing. The production is not.

Junior Caminero — 3B
3B | 2026 Salary: $2 | Contract Year 2 | 2025: 519.5 FPts, 3.37 FPts/G

A third baseman with one of the most explosive fantasy profiles in the sport at two dollars a year. Caminero played 154 games in 2025 and produced 519.5 total fantasy points — third on the team behind only Woo and Alonso. He's entering Year 2 at $2, escalating to $5 in 2027. At that price, he's the best positional player bargain on any roster in this league. If this contract has a flaw, it's that it makes every other $2 contract look overpriced by comparison.

Bryan Woo — SP
SP | 2026 Salary: $10 | Contract Year 4 | 2025: 554 FPts, 18.5 FPts/G

The team's leading fantasy scorer and the most dominant starter in the league by per-game average. Woo made 30 starts in 2025 and topped out at 554 fantasy points at a rate of 18.47 per start. He's entering Year 4 at $10 — still a strong value for a pitcher of his caliber — though the contract creeps to $15 in 2027, when the salary justification math begins to tighten. For now, Woo sits alongside Skenes as one of the two best rotational assets in the game at their respective price points.

Pete Alonso — 1B
1B | 2026 Salary: $40 | Contract Year 5 | 2025: 535 FPts, 3.3 FPts/G

The biggest contract on the roster, and the one that generates the most strategic conversation. Alonso played all 162 games and produced 535 fantasy points — strong production at any salary. But $40 in Year 5, escalating to $45, is approaching the ceiling of defensible keeping. A 30-year-old first baseman who cannot be deployed anywhere other than first base is the definition of a single-position dependency. Alonso's bat is real. The trajectory of his contract is the question Arizona will face with increasing urgency in 2027 and 2028.

Beyond the headliners, the supporting cast earns its keep. George Springer ($30, Year 5) delivered 525.5 points across 140 games — legitimate production from a legitimate outfielder. But like Alonso, Springer's contract is in its expensive years, and at 36, the question of how long that production holds is real. He's entering Year 5 at $30 with a $35 price tag looming in 2027. His dynasty value is declining while his keeper cost is not. Arizona will need to make a decision on Springer before this contract year becomes the one that prompted regret.

Jesus Luzardo ($18, Year 5) contributed 476 points in 32 starts — a reliable front-of-rotation piece at a cost that's pushing toward its ceiling. The same Year 5 concern applies: $18 becomes $23 in 2027. Jhoan Duran ($15, Year 5) produced 400.5 points from the bullpen despite being limited to 72 games, a testament to his per-appearance dominance. But at $15 heading to $20, elite relievers begin to look shaky as dynasty commitments when they age into their late 20s.

Salvador Perez ($18, Year 3) remains one of the best-producing catchers in the league with 397.5 points in 155 games, and he provides dual eligibility at catcher and first base — a valuable roster construction tool. At 35 years old, Perez is the veteran catcher who gives elite production one more season before the physical grind of catching starts to show in the numbers. Spencer Torkelson ($9, Year 2) has quietly emerged as one of the better auction-value stories of the past two seasons: signed at $8 in 2025, he returned 416.5 points in 155 games and enters 2026 at $9. The concern is that he's eligible only at first base, a position that's already crowded with Alonso and Perez.

The young value tier rounds out the picture. Taylor Ward ($5, Year 3) posted 461.5 points in 157 games at a steal price. Brandon Lowe ($8, Year 3) provides second base eligibility alongside 373.5 points in 134 games — reliable depth at a reasonable cost. Colton Cowser ($2, Year 2) and Jakob Marsee ($2, Year 2) are outfield depth pieces locked in at the cheapest possible keeper prices. Cam Schlittler ($2, Year 2) and Luis Morales ($2, Year 2) are young starting pitching prospects who have shown brief but promising production — Schlittler at 12.21 FPts/G over 14 starts, Morales at 11.55 FPts/G over 10 starts. Both are lottery tickets on a roster that can afford them.

The total keeper salary of $226 leaves $34 for the FA auction — seventh-highest of any team in the league, which puts Arizona in the uncomfortable middle: too little budget to dominate the auction, too much roster quality to be desperate. The 2026 salary chart shows the team's contract commitments are heavily weighted toward their Year 4 and Year 5 players, which means the back half of this decade will require hard decisions. For 2026, though, the value pile at the top of the roster is extraordinary.

The Minors

Farm Report

The Arizona farm system in 2026 is one of the more legitimately impressive collections in the league — a mix of genuine top-prospect talent, players who have already contributed at the major league level, and deep-system depth pieces that keep the MiLB slots maximally utilized. With nine of ten MiLB spots filled, every slot is earning its keep. Farm Grade: B+

Samuel Basallo (C, 21) is the crown jewel. Basallo is a consensus top-10 prospect in all of professional baseball — a 21-year-old catcher with power, contact ability, and the defensive profile to stick behind the plate long-term. He appeared in 31 games in 2025 and produced 55 fantasy points — early major league time for a player who won't stay in the minors for long. He represents the most exciting dynasty asset on a roster already rich with dynasty assets. If Basallo develops as projected, he becomes the replacement piece for an aging Salvador Perez and a franchise cornerstone for the next decade.

Robby Snelling (LHP, 22) is a consensus top-38 prospect — a left-handed pitching arm who hasn't yet appeared in a major league game but is rapidly climbing toward that opportunity. Both major scouting systems agree on his ranking, which gives his prospect status genuine credibility. He's a long-term stash who could make an impact in 2027 or 2028.

Braden Montgomery (OF, 22) is the most contested prospect in the system. One major service has him as a top-20 player in baseball; another slots him in the mid-60s. That spread indicates real disagreement about his hit tool projection and long-term positional fit. The conservative estimate still makes him a consensus top-42 outfield prospect. The aggressive estimate makes him one of the most undervalued assets in the game. Arizona drafted him in Round 2 and will let the reality play out.

Then there are the two players who probably shouldn't be in the minor league pool at all. Nick Kurtz (1B, 22) produced 428.5 fantasy points across 117 games in 2025 — that's the production of a legitimate major league contributor, not a developmental stash. He was the 9th overall pick in the MiLB draft and has clearly outgrown his minor league designation. At $0 salary in a MiLB slot, he represents extraordinary hidden value, but the clock is ticking on how long that arrangement makes strategic sense. Similarly, Kyle Manzardo (1B, 25) delivered 323.5 points in 142 games. At 25 with no meaningful prospect rankings remaining, Manzardo has aged out of the traditional prospect window. His value is as an active contributor, not a development stash.

Luke Keaschall (OF, 23) showed 168.5 points in 49 games — a productive sample that suggests he could slot into the active roster if needed. Alex Freeland (24) sits at a consensus 170th-ranked prospect — deep organizational depth with meaningful upside at the right price point (which is $0). Ben Joyce (RP, 25) has appeared in just 5 games but carries legitimate high-leverage bullpen upside if his arm holds. Emiliano Teodo (25) is the deep system lottery ticket — ranked well below the top 500 by one service, above 350 by another. A true developmental stash.

Call-Up Timeline: The March 15 call-up decision creates immediate strategic implications. Kurtz and Manzardo are both active contributors with no remaining prospect eligibility — calling them up to the active roster before March 15 would open two MiLB draft slots and allow Arizona to enter the upcoming draft with three picks instead of one. That decision should be a near-automatic one. Keaschall could see active roster time in April or May if an injury creates a need. Basallo projects as a mid-season call-up candidate at best; more likely 2027. Snelling, Joyce, and Montgomery are long-term stashes through 2026.

Samuel Basallo at 21 years old, consensus top-10 prospect in baseball, stashed in Arizona's MiLB slot at $0. Every other team in this league should be jealous.
Pre-Auction Bubble

Drop Watch

The defending champions enter 2026 with 20 confirmed keepers at $226 — which means $34 for the auction. That's enough to make targeted additions but not enough to dominate the bidding on marquee free agents. The question before the March 22 auction is whether Arizona makes any final cuts to buy themselves more flexibility. These are the names under the microscope:

Shane Baz — SP, $12, Year 4: The most compelling drop case on the roster. Baz produced 309 fantasy points in 31 starts, but his per-game average of 9.97 is below the standard expected from a starter carrying a $12 contract in Year 4. His league-relative performance at the SP position places him squarely in the below-average tier, and the free agent pool has starting options — Zack Wheeler (446.5 FPts), Kevin Gausman (451.5 FPts) — who outproduced him substantially in 2025. Dropping Baz frees $12 in auction budget and opens a rotation spot for a superior arm. The argument for keeping him is organizational familiarity and health. The argument against him is that the upgrade path is available and the budget savings are meaningful. This cut makes sense.

Taj Bradley — SP, $10, Year 4: Bradley appeared in only 27 starts and delivered 261.5 points — a rate of 9.69 per start that looks serviceable until you measure it against what $10 can buy at auction. The rotation already has Woo, Skenes, and Luzardo as anchors. Schlittler and Morales are $2 starters with upside. Bradley at $10 heading to $15 next year is increasingly hard to justify as the fifth arm on a staff that could use the auction savings elsewhere. He's another strong cut candidate.

Jurickson Profar — OF, $9, Year 3: Profar produced 276.5 points in just 80 games — a rate of 3.46 per game that is functional but unremarkable from an outfield corner. He carries OF eligibility only, meaning he doesn't add positional flexibility to a roster that already has Springer, Ward, Cowser, and Marsee in the outfield picture. At $9 heading to $14 next year, Profar is an overpay that compounds. Releasing him would provide meaningful budget room without touching any core piece.

Trevor Megill — RP, $17, Year 2: The championship season's biggest waiver-wire hero is also the roster's most complicated 2026 contract. Megill is a reliever at $17 heading to $20 — a price point where any injury or regression renders the contract untenable immediately. His 2025 contribution was real, but the rate of value delivery (20.1 FPts per dollar) is below the standard Arizona's best contracts set. If Nichols wants to free auction budget, Megill is the most salary-significant cut available without touching a cornerstone.

The calculus is straightforward: cutting Baz, Bradley, and Profar alone would free $31 — nearly doubling the available auction budget to $65. That's the difference between being a price-taker at auction and actually competing for one of the top available players. The roster has enough depth to absorb those losses. The decision is whether to run the same championship formula or invest the budget in upgrades before the competition responds to Arizona's title defense.

Auction Strategy

Free Agent Auction

Here is the cold reality of Arizona's 2026 auction position: at $34, they are seventh out of 18 teams in available budget. Philadelphia ($199), Toronto ($185), Chicago ($167), Atlanta ($135), and Washington ($129) all have resources that will allow them to simply outbid Arizona on any player they target. The top two names on every fantasy manager's wish list — Shohei Ohtani (680.5 FPts) and Juan Soto (661.5 FPts) — will be claimed by teams spending in the triple digits. Arizona shouldn't even enter those bidding wars.

What they should do is address the most glaring structural flaw in their entire roster construction: there is no shortstop. Scan the eligible positions column for every confirmed 2026 keeper. No SS appears anywhere on this roster. Alonso is at first base, Torkelson is at first base, Caminero is at third base, Lowe is at second base. The infield is well-stocked at every position except the one that arguably matters most in dynasty leagues. This is a priority need that should eat a significant portion of whatever budget Arizona brings to the auction.

Primary Targets (from the confirmed FA pool):

Dansby Swanson (SS, 409 FPts, 2025) is the most direct fit. He provides shortstop eligibility and produced at a rate that would slot comfortably behind the team's elite contributors. Bid range: $14–18. Gleyber Torres (2B/SS, 404.5 FPts) adds middle infield depth across two positions. His 2B eligibility also gives Arizona redundancy behind Lowe. Bid range: $12–16. Oneil Cruz (SS, 353 FPts) is the high-upside alternative — a player with a remarkable dynasty profile and the kind of power for a shortstop that changes games. His 2025 production was below Swanson's, but the dynasty ceiling is considerably higher. Bid range: $10–14.

Beyond the SS need, Arizona's rotation is strong enough that starting pitching isn't a desperation priority — but if the budget allows after addressing shortstop, Kevin Gausman (451.5 FPts, SP) or Zack Wheeler (446.5 FPts, SP) would provide genuine starting depth that outperforms what Bradley and Baz have offered. These are also possible targets for teams that could then trade them — Yoshinobu Yamamoto (496.5 FPts) and Rafael Devers (3B, 522 FPts) round out the names worth tracking.

At catcher, the Yainer Diaz option (C, 327.5 FPts) is worth monitoring as a secondary target if Arizona wants depth behind Salvador Perez. J.T. Realmuto (C, 295.5 FPts) is a veteran fallback.

Secondary Targets (from likely cut candidates): Several players held by other teams are reasonable cut candidates who could surface in the FA pool before March 22. Jose Ramirez (3B, SDP, $71/Year 5) might be released by a San Diego team carrying a severely negative auction budget — if he clears, a bid in the $40–55 range could land a transformative asset, though Arizona would need to cut salary elsewhere to make the math work. Logan Webb (SP, SEA, $61/Year 4) is a premium rotation option that could theoretically free up if Seattle's $281 keeper salary forces their hand. Chris Sale (SP, NYY, $55) is another possibility if New York trims from their overbudget position.

The honest assessment of Arizona's auction strategy: they should trim the bubble names to build toward $50–65 in available budget, attack the shortstop market with conviction, and add one quality starting pitcher if the price is right. Unused budget does not carry over. Spending $34 and leaving $20 on the table would be a mismanagement of a championship roster's resources.

MiLB Draft

MiLB Draft Outlook

The defending champions pick last. That's the price of success in TSDL's MiLB draft, where draft priority #18 means Arizona will be working with whatever the other 17 teams passed on in each round. There's no shame in this — every champion faces it — but it demands a specific drafting philosophy: target players with upside, volatility, or injury risk that caused them to fall, rather than expecting consensus top-tier talent to be available late.

Before the draft opens on March 15, Arizona should seriously consider calling up both Nick Kurtz and Kyle Manzardo to the active roster. Both players have outgrown the MiLB designation based on their 2025 production. Moving them to the active roster would open two additional MiLB draft slots, giving Arizona three picks in the upcoming draft instead of one. That's a meaningful difference in organizational building capacity at the #18 draft position.

With three picks (assuming the call-ups), Arizona's draft targets should prioritize: high-ceiling pitching arms who have fallen due to injury history or command volatility; outfield depth behind the aging Springer with dynasty upside; and any prospect with a genuine top-100 ranking who has been bypassed due to service time or organizational concerns. The system already has Basallo, Snelling, and Montgomery as its top-three prospects — the draft is about adding depth and lottery tickets, not anchors. One prospect who checks the "fell due to risk" criteria in the high-100s range would be the ideal pickup at the end of each round.

Season Prediction

2026 Season Prediction

Projected Record: 12–7 to 14–5

Projected Seed: #3–6

Playoff Probability: Very High

Championship Probability: High

Ceiling: Back-to-back champion, driven by Skenes/Woo/Caminero as the league's most efficient roster construction

Floor: Auction budget limitations leave the SS hole unaddressed, Springer decline accelerates, and a 9–10 seed with a first-round playoff exit

Here's the bold take: Arizona wins the 2026 TSDL championship.

That's not a hedge. The Diamondbacks have Paul Skenes at $5 and Junior Caminero at $2. Those two contracts alone represent the most asymmetric dynasty value in the league. Skenes is a top-three pitcher in baseball at a price that belongs on a rookie minimum. Caminero is a 3B producing 520 fantasy points while costing less than a bag of sunflower seeds. When you add Bryan Woo — the actual points leader in 2025 — at $10 in Year 4, you have the three best value-to-production contracts in the game on a single roster. That kind of construction advantage doesn't disappear when the calendar flips.

The core offense is durable. Pete Alonso played 162 games. George Springer played 140. Spencer Torkelson played 155. Taylor Ward played 157. This isn't a lineup that misses time; it's one that grinds through seasons. The hitting split at 50.9/49.1 suggests balance that will survive the inevitable variance in pitching performance. The rotation — Woo, Skenes, Luzardo, and two competition spots — is among the league's top three.

If the auction goes well — if Nichols trims salary, builds budget, and addresses the SS gap with Swanson or Torres — the 2026 Diamondbacks project as a legitimate top-4 seed and the most dangerous team in any postseason bracket. The farm system's depth means mid-season upgrades are available if needed. Samuel Basallo's development adds a long-term backstop behind Perez. Nick Kurtz's potential call-up adds a genuine starter to a roster that doesn't need him desperately but would happily take 428 more points this season.

The floor scenario involves Springer's production falling off at $30, the auction budget not stretching to cover the SS need, and one of the aging Year 5 contracts becoming a dead-weight anchor mid-season. In that scenario, Arizona is a very good team that finishes 10th and makes the playoffs as a low seed. That's the floor. Given what they just did as the eighth seed, even the floor version of this team is genuinely dangerous in October.

But the ceiling? The ceiling is a dynasty. A runner-up in Year 1 despite going 16-2 — the best regular-season record in league history — only to be denied the title. A title in Year 4 as an 8-seed, beating the first, second, and fourth seeds in succession. A repeat in Year 5 as a genuine favorite for the first time in three years. The 2022 Diamondbacks were historically dominant and had history stolen from them in the Finals. The 2025 Diamondbacks took it back from the least likely position possible. The 2026 Diamondbacks are something rarer: a defending champion with an improving roster, extraordinary young talent locked in at bargain prices, and an owner who has demonstrated the ability to make the right moves when they count. In TSDL, that combination doesn't come along often. When it does, you pay attention.

Cote Nichols built something real in Arizona. Now comes the test of whether it lasts.
