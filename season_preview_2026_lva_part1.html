2026 TSDL Season Preview: Las Vegas Athletics — Part 1: The Season That Was

By Ken Rosenthal — 2026-02-20 — 12 min read

Introduction

The Price of Empire

Two seasons in the TSDL. Thirty wins. Eight losses. One championship ring. The kind of launch trajectory that makes rival owners quietly check the league settings to confirm the competition is actually fair.

And yet here we are in February 2026, and Mike DiMauro has just done the unthinkable: he released Juan Soto. Not traded him. Not packaged him for a haul of prospects and future assets. Released him outright — the player who posted 661.5 fantasy points in 2025, the second-highest total on the entire Las Vegas Athletics roster and one of the best individual performances in the league. The reason is brutally simple: keeping Soto at his $95 escalation cost would have consumed every meaningful dollar of LVA's auction budget and left the team with negative flexibility heading into 2026. DiMauro made a coldly rational decision. The core is strong enough without him. He might be right. He might be spectacularly wrong. We will find out either way before the summer is over.

The context matters here. When DiMauro entered this league two years ago, the Las Vegas Athletics had one goal: build fast, win now, and worry about sustainability later. He won the championship in Year 1. He finished 15-4 again in Year 2 and ran deep into the playoffs before losing to the eventual champion in the conference round. Two 15-4 seasons. A 30-8 all-time record, a 78.9% win rate, and a trophy. By any objective measure, this is the most successful two-year stretch any expansion team has put together in recent TSDL memory.

But years of aggressive trading, big auction spends, and roster maximization have consequences. By the end of 2025, LVA was carrying $359 in active salary across 26 players — a payroll built for winning right now, with little margin for error. The offseason reset was inevitable. The only question was how deep it would cut. The answer: deep enough to lose Soto, but not deep enough to fundamentally change who this team is. The star core is intact. The farm is thriving. The auction budget is nearly nonexistent. DiMauro is betting on the horses he has. Let's see if he's right to.

2025 Activity

How the Machine Was Built

The Las Vegas Athletics entered the 2025 calendar year with the 17th pick in each round of the MiLB draft — a draft position earned by finishing near the top of the league the prior season. DiMauro used those picks methodically, adding Felnin Celesten (Round 1, 17th overall), Jaison Chourio (Round 2), George Klassen (Round 3), Ralphy Velazquez (Round 4), and Emil Morales (Round 5) to a minor league system that already held five retained keepers: Heston Kjerstad, Emmanuel Rodriguez, Max Clark, Thomas White, and Jackson Jobe. Ten prospects across ten rounds — a comprehensive restocking effort for a team that clearly isn't planning to plateau.

Two months into the regular season, four of those ten names were already currency in trades that brought back MLB-ready talent. That's not mismanagement. That's a team willing to convert future assets into present-day firepower, which is exactly what championship-caliber rosters do.

The 2025 FA auction was restrained by design, not neglect. With roughly $70 available after keeper commitments, DiMauro spent exactly that — a clean, efficient deployment. The headline acquisition was Manny Machado at $34, who went on to deliver 486.5 FPts across 159 games. That is a textbook auction return: a productive veteran at a price that looks like a bargain by season's end. Marcus Semien ($23) and Shane Bieber ($6) rounded out the major bids. Semien was dealt away within weeks. Bieber was limited by injury to just seven starts. Neither outcome was catastrophic — Machado more than carried the auction's weight.

The in-season trade activity is where the identity of the 2025 Las Vegas Athletics truly crystallized. It started quietly in April: Aaron Nola ($31, Year 4) and Sal Frelick to Chicago for Riley Greene ($12) and Erick Fedde — clearing aging rotation salary for a younger outfield piece. Marcus Semien went to Philadelphia for Reese Olson ($6), a young righty with real upside. Then, on May 9, DiMauro executed the deal that changed everything.

He shipped George Klassen, Luis Garcia, Riley Greene, and Hayden Wesneski to Boston in exchange for Vance Honeycutt, Carlos Rodon ($16, Year 4), and Rafael Devers ($49, Year 4). Four middling assets for two established major league starters. The trade gave DiMauro Rodon — who produced 541.5 FPts in 33 starts and anchored the rotation all season — plus Devers as an asset to immediately flip.

He flipped him eight days later. Devers and Spencer Steer went to Cincinnati in exchange for Luis Gil and Juan Soto ($90, Year 3). Read that again: DiMauro acquired Juan Soto — one of the top five fantasy hitters in baseball — for the price of a third baseman he had held for eight days and a spare outfielder. It is the kind of move that defines dynasty careers. Soto delivered 661.5 FPts in 160 games. The offense became a different animal.

The final significant move came on August 1, when DiMauro traded Lawrence Butler ($2) to Cincinnati for Jacob deGrom ($28, Year 2). DeGrom, perpetually one step from the injured list, pitched 30 starts and posted 477.5 FPts at a 15.9 FP/G — one of the elite per-start marks in the league. Lawrence Butler for Jacob deGrom, mid-August, with a $26 salary differential in DiMauro's favor. That's buy-low artistry.

The full scope of LVA's 2025 activity — ten trades in a single season, including two back-to-back with the same Boston team and a complex three-way asset chain through Cincinnati — earned a high grade in execution. The only reason it doesn't earn perfect marks is the outcome: they made the championship round and lost to the eventual champion by 34 points. So close.

In-Season Strategy Grade: A

Season Analysis

2025 Season in Review

The numbers tell the story of a team that started slowly, found its footing, and became one of the two or three most dominant rosters in the league by midsummer. LVA opened 0-1 — a 395-458.5 loss to Seattle in Week 1 — and then proceeded to go 15-3 over their remaining 18 regular season matchups. By Week 5, they had climbed to #1 in power rankings. By Week 7, they were 6-1. They never fell below third place after Week 9.

League Avg: 3.9 — top tier

More hit-dominant than 2024 (52.4% / 47.6%)

The team's offensive production — 271 HR, 818 RBI, 181 SB, 918 R, and a Silver Slugger Award — was the engine of everything good about 2025 LVA. The pitching was above average but relied heavily on a few elite starts from Rodon, deGrom, and Ryan. When the starters were healthy, the team was unstoppable. When they weren't — Week 11 produced just 207 points against St. Louis, the worst single-week performance in team history — the holes showed. The 267-268 Week 12 loss to Atlanta, settled by a single point margin, was the closest DiMauro's team has ever come to a result that genuinely changed the playoff seeding picture.

The top performers were exactly what you'd expect from a roster assembled through calculated aggression: Aaron Judge (693 FPts, 152 games), Juan Soto (661.5 FPts, 160 games), Francisco Lindor (574.5 FPts, 160 games), Corbin Carroll (551.5 FPts, 143 games), Carlos Rodon (541.5 FPts, 33 starts). Five players over 500 FPts. That depth of production is how you go 15-4.

Then came the playoffs. LVA dispatched Toronto in the divisional round with authority, 725.5 to 614.5, as Judge and Soto combined for what amounted to a week-long demonstration of elite fantasy production. They entered the conference round as the second seed against Arizona — the eighth seed, 11-8 on the year, a team that hadn't been considered a serious championship contender by anyone with a pulse. Arizona won, 762 to 728. A 34-point margin over two weeks. The Diamondbacks went on to claim the title.

The 8-seed Arizona Diamondbacks beat the 2-seed Las Vegas Athletics in the conference round, then won the championship. That 34-point margin is the difference between a dynasty and a footnote — and DiMauro knows it.

LVA salvaged third place with a dominating 739.5-484 win over Seattle in the consolation round. It is a result that speaks to the team's genuine caliber — they outscored their opponent by 255 points when fully locked in — but it is cold comfort when the trophy went to a team they should have beaten. Two seasons in the TSDL. One title. One third-place finish where a first-place finish was absolutely within reach. The hunger should be ferocious heading into 2026.

Offseason

The Great Simplification

Ten players released. For a team that won 15 games, that is not a quiet offseason — it is a structural reset. The 2025 LVA roster carried $359 in salary across 26 active players. The 2026 version will carry $249 across 16 active keepers. Something had to give, and DiMauro gave a lot.

Start with the obvious: Juan Soto, released despite 661.5 FPts and a case as one of the two best hitters on the roster. The $95 escalation cost for 2026 made this decision a matter of arithmetic rather than analysis. Keeping Soto would have pushed LVA's keeper salary past $340 before a single minor league player was counted, leaving an auction budget of negative eighty-something dollars. Something had to give. Soto gave. Whether this decision looks brilliant or catastrophic in October 2026 will be the central narrative of the season. What is undeniable is that DiMauro chose to go into 2026 without the league's most feared hitter rather than compromise the financial structure his team needs to operate.

Bryan Reynolds ($26 → $31, Year 5) was a logical release: 358.5 FPts at a 2.3 FP/G production rate, below average for an outfielder entering a $31 salary in the fifth year of an escalating contract. The math on aging OF production at that price range never ends well. Raisel Iglesias ($19 → $24, Year 5) is a similar story: a reliable closer who delivered 353.5 FPts across 70 appearances, but entering Year 5 at $24 for bullpen production that the FA market can replicate at a fraction of the cost. At that salary, you're paying for what a reliever was, not what he will be.

Gleyber Torres ($6 → $9, Year 3) was the most surprising of the non-Soto cuts. Torres posted 404.5 FPts across 145 games — genuinely productive 2B output — and the $9 escalation cost is affordable by most standards. But with Gunnar Henderson providing elite SS production and Manny Machado anchoring third base, Torres was positional surplus. The $9 is better deployed in a different direction.

Reese Olson ($6 → $11, Year 4) is the cut that will generate the most second-guessing. Acquired in April via the Semien trade, Olson pitched to a 11.0 FP/G rate across 13 starts — elite numbers on a limited sample. Cutting an arm that young, with that ceiling, at $11 in Year 4 frees meaningful auction dollars but surrenders a pitcher who likely has a productive MLB career ahead of him. The TSDL free agent pool will move quickly once his name hits the wire.

The remaining five cuts — Bailey Ober, Royce Lewis, Clayton Kershaw, Shawn Armstrong, and Landen Roupp — represent depth clearing without meaningful controversy. Ober was below-average SP production in 27 starts heading to $12 in Year 4. Lewis produced just 241 FPts across 106 games with injury-limited durability and a below-average production profile at 3B. Kershaw at $2 and Armstrong at $2 were end-of-career and versatile-but-expendable pieces, respectively. Roupp, at $2 in Year 2, showed promise (203 FPts, 9.2 FP/G in 22 starts) but not enough to hold a roster spot over other options. Each cut individually is defensible. Together, they represent DiMauro's willingness to accept short-term production losses in exchange for structural flexibility — even when the budget the cuts buy is only $11.
