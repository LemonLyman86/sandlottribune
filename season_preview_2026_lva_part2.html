2026 TSDL Season Preview: Las Vegas Athletics — Part 2: The Year Ahead

By Ken Rosenthal — 2026-02-20 — 12 min read

The Roster

The Core — Returning Players

The 2026 keeper salary chart above tells a story DiMauro knows by heart: this is a roster concentrated in premium, aging contracts. The commitments are real, the talent is elite, and the window is open — but not indefinitely. With $249 in keeper salary and $11 in auction budget, every dollar of the retained roster must justify its cost.

Let's start with the cornerstones.

Aaron Judge — OF
2026 Cost: $39  |  Contract Year: 5  |  2025 FPts: 693.0

The franchise anchor and the best fantasy performer on the roster. Judge posted 693 FPts across 152 games in 2025 — a 4.6 FP/G average that is elite at any position. At $39 entering Year 5, he sits in the expensive tier of the contract structure. The escalation math is real: $39 now, $44 in 2027, $49 in 2028, and climbing. But at 33 years old and still producing at a historic pace, the bill doesn't come due until the production starts to slip — and it hasn't slipped yet. For now, Judge at $39 is the foundation everything else is built around.

Corbin Carroll — OF
2026 Cost: $10  |  Contract Year: 4  |  2025 FPts: 551.5

This might be the single best contract in the Las Vegas Athletics stable, and it isn't particularly close. Carroll produced 551.5 FPts in 143 games at a 3.9 FP/G average — third-best on the roster — while costing just $10 in Year 4. At 23 years old, he is on the ascending arc of what projects to be a long, valuable career. The $10 price tag is almost embarrassing. DiMauro knows this won't last forever — by Year 7, Carroll will cost $35+ — but right now this is a cornerstone player at a bargain-bin price, the best-value contract in a rotation of good contracts.

Francisco Lindor — SS
2026 Cost: $34  |  Contract Year: 5  |  2025 FPts: 574.5

Lindor is the floor of this roster — dependable, durable, and one of the top shortstops in fantasy baseball. He appeared in all 160 games and delivered 574.5 FPts at 3.6 FP/G, a number that becomes even more impressive when you consider it was the third-best individual performance on a roster that also included Judge and Soto. Like Judge, Lindor is in Year 5 and the salary clock is ticking. The $34 is justified for what he produces; by 2028 or 2029, DiMauro will face the same decision he just made with Soto. But that conversation is years away.

Gunnar Henderson — SS
2026 Cost: $6  |  Contract Year: 3  |  2025 FPts: 461.5

Six dollars. That is what DiMauro is paying for a player who produced 461.5 FPts in 154 games — one of the elite statistical profiles in the entire league. Henderson is 25 years old, eligible at SS, and entering Year 3 of a contract that costs $6. The next escalation won't happen until 2027 (to $11), and after that $16, $21... the upside window here is enormous. Having both Lindor and Henderson at the same position creates a roster luxury that most owners can only dream about. The only challenge is ensuring both are in the lineup every day, which is a very good problem to have.

Beyond the four cornerstones, the supporting cast is equally legitimate. Jacob deGrom ($31, Year 3) is the wild card: 477.5 FPts in 30 starts at 15.9 FP/G, as good as any starter in the league when healthy. The question is always whether he's healthy, and at $31 with no auction budget to absorb a dead salary, a deGrom injury has real consequences. Carlos Rodon ($21, Year 5) delivered 541.5 FPts and has been one of the two best pitchers on this roster. Year 5 brings escalation concerns, but $21 for an ace-level arm is still manageable. Manny Machado ($35, Year 2) was a $34 auction steal who produced 486.5 FPts in 159 games — the best single-season auction return in LVA history. Year 2 at $35 is a fair price for what Machado is.

Joe Ryan ($16, Year 5) was quietly exceptional: 439 FPts in 31 starts, the most durable of the rotation starters. Logan Gilbert ($21, Year 5) provided 323.5 FPts in 25 starts. George Kirby ($6, Year 3) showed genuine potential with 296.5 FPts in 23 starts at 12.9 FP/G — another cheap contract with real upside. Kenley Jansen ($7, Year 2) rounds out the bullpen with 349.5 FPts across 62 appearances.

The one contract that generates genuine unease is Shane Bieber ($7, Year 2). Bieber appeared in just 7 games in 2025, likely due to injury — but the 108.5 FPts he collected in those starts produced a 15.5 FP/G rate that ranks among the best in the league. If Bieber pitches 25 starts in 2026, $7 is an outrageous bargain. If the injury history repeats itself, he's $7 of dead salary on a team with no financial cushion. This is the bet that DiMauro has to decide whether he's willing to let ride.

The Minors

Farm Report

Here is where the Las Vegas Athletics separate themselves from a team simply riding an aging core into the sunset. Nine of ten minor league slots are filled with legitimate organizational depth, and the headliner is one of the best prospects in all of baseball.

Max Clark (21, OF) is the crown jewel. A consensus top-eight prospect in baseball — one major scouting system has him ranked sixth overall, another places him sixth as well, and the average of available rankings puts him eighth — Clark is the kind of talent that dynasty rosters are built around. He is 21 years old, physically projectable, and the only question is how quickly his bat translates at the major league level. DiMauro faces a consequential call-up decision before the March 15 MiLB deadline: bring Clark to the active roster now and get his production for $0 in 2026, at the cost of starting his salary escalation clock, or leave him in the minors and preserve the flexibility of a future call-up. For a team with $11 in auction budget, adding a top-eight prospect to the active roster for free is an extraordinarily appealing option.

Emmanuel Rodriguez (22, OF) is a genuine top-prospect caliber arm in his own right. Consensus rankings place him between 44th and 67th overall depending on the source — a range that reflects some analytical disagreement but unanimously confirms real talent. At 22 with significant organizational investment behind him, Rodriguez is the most likely mid-season callup if Clark graduates to the active roster. Felnin Celesten (20) was the first-round MiLB pick from last year's draft: one system places him top-48 overall, another closer to top-103, with a consensus near 76th. The divergence reflects a younger prospect whose ceiling remains contested — but both systems see meaningful upside in a 20-year-old shortstop with clear plus tools.

Jackson Jobe (23) appeared in ten games in 2025, flashing the talent that made him an organizational priority. At 23, he sits at the threshold of prospect eligibility aging out, but ten major-league appearances at a productive rate signal that a full-season contribution could come in the near future. Ralphy Velazquez (20) is a contested evaluation: one major system loves him (top-60 range), another is considerably cooler (top-161), and the consensus of 110th reflects genuine uncertainty about a high-variance young bat. Emil Morales (19), a consensus top-72 teenager, rounds out the upper tier — long-term upside with no immediate timeline.

Rounding out the system is Eduardo Tait (19, #148 consensus) — intriguing as a teenager with legitimate long-term projection, but no near-term timeline to the active roster.

Potential 2026 Call-Up Timeline:

  • Before Auction (March 15): Max Clark — the clearest candidate. If DiMauro decides to activate him now, it changes this team's 2026 offensive ceiling dramatically.
  • Early Season (April–May): Jackson Jobe, if spring goes well. Emmanuel Rodriguez, if Clark is already up.
  • Mid-Season: Emmanuel Rodriguez (if not called up earlier), Felnin Celesten if organizational timelines accelerate.
  • Long-Term Stashes: Emil Morales, Ralphy Velazquez, Eduardo Tait.

Farm System Grade: B+

The grade would be an A if this system were attached to a rebuilding team. In the context of a squad that already won a championship, carrying a top-eight prospect, a top-60 arm, and legitimate secondary depth is remarkable. The farm supplements the core rather than replacing it — that is exactly where you want to be as a dynasty contender.

Pre-Auction Watch

Drop Watch

With $11 in auction budget, every dollar of active keeper salary that can be recovered deserves examination. The good news is that DiMauro already made the hard cuts in the offseason. The questionable pieces that remain are, mostly, cheap. But cheap adds up.

Joe Musgrove — SP — $2 (Year 2)
The data here is unambiguous. Musgrove appeared on LVA's active roster via a mid-April trade from Seattle and posted zero 2025 statistics. Not a poor performance — zero performance, almost certainly due to injury. There is no production to weigh against the $2 cost, and there is no reason to believe a healthy 2026 Musgrove is worth more than what the auction market will offer in his spot. Drop him. Spend the extra $2 on something real. This decision should take about thirty seconds.

Shane Bieber — SP — $7 (Year 2)
This one is genuinely hard. Bieber's seven starts produced a 15.5 FP/G — elite per-start output that serves as evidence of what he could do in a healthy season. But Bieber's injury history is a pattern now, not an anomaly. For a team with $11 in auction budget and no financial safety net, $7 on an arm that has a legitimate chance of contributing five or fewer starts is a real risk. Keep him if you're a believer. Cut him if you need the budget. There is no wrong answer, but there is a financially prudent one.

Kenley Jansen — RP — $7 (Year 2)
Jansen delivered 349.5 FPts across 62 appearances in 2025 — a genuine, productive closing performance from a proven veteran. At 38 years old entering 2026, the long-term dynasty profile here is essentially zero. DiMauro already cut Raisel Iglesias, who provided similar bullpen value at a higher price. Jansen at $7 is the last defined closer on this active roster after that cut, which gives him positional value. But if the decision is between Jansen at $7 and a more promising FA acquisition at the auction for $7-10, the math favors making a move. This is a conversation worth having out loud.

Ivan Herrera — UT — $5 (Year 3)
Herrera posted 330 FPts in 107 games and is eligible at utility only — no primary position listed. In a league where premium positional eligibility drives value, UT-only is the thinnest possible designation. His long-term dynasty profile is limited, and with Tyler Soderstrom ($5, eligible at 1B and OF) already filling a comparable backup bat role with better positional coverage, Herrera becomes the most expendable active-roster piece on the team. Dropping both Musgrove and Herrera raises the auction budget from $11 to $18. That's a more workable figure and the minimum floor DiMauro should target before auction day.

Auction Strategy

Free Agent Auction — Making $11 Count

Eleven dollars. That is the gap between LVA's $249 keeper salary and the $260 FA auction cap. For context: the Philadelphia Phillies have $199 to spend. Toronto has $185. Chicago has $167. These are the teams that will arrive at the auction table with the ability to outbid anyone in the room on any player they want. LVA will arrive with what amounts to a single competitive bid.

Las Vegas has $11 to work with in the auction. Their strategy cannot be winning the bidding war — it has to be identifying the one player who slips through. In every auction, someone always does.

The confirmed free agent pool for 2026 is loaded with talent that LVA cannot afford. Players like Paul Skenes (596 FPts, was held by Arizona), Garrett Crochet (586.5 FPts), and Elly De La Cruz (524.5 FPts) will command $40-70+ bids from teams with massive budgets. DiMauro can watch from the sideline. His targets need to exist in a different part of the board.

The realistic opportunities at $11 — or up to $18-20 if Musgrove and Herrera are released before the auction:

Hunter Brown (SP) — Brown produced 469 FPts in 2025, genuine starting pitcher value. As a former Chicago Cubs keeper now entering the free agent pool, a $5 current salary at Year 3 suggests he may move in auction at a price in the $10-15 range. LVA, fresh off releasing Olson, Ober, Armstrong, and Roupp from their rotation depth, has a real need for a reliable arm. Brown is the highest-priority realistic target.

Jackson Chourio (OF) — 478 FPts, $5 current salary at Year 3. The St. Louis Cardinals' decision to release this outfield producer opens a legitimate opportunity for a team that just lost Soto and Reynolds from their OF. Chourio will attract significant auction competition, but if he slips to $10-12, that is a realistic bid range with an enhanced budget.

Cade Smith (RP) — 474.5 FPts at $2 (Year 2) for a Texas Rangers arm entering the free agent market. LVA needs bullpen depth after releasing Iglesias. Smith provides the kind of high-leverage value that justifies a $7-10 bid, and the small current salary suggests he might move lower than his production warrants if the auction room is distracted by bigger names.

Vinnie Pasquantino (1B/OF) — 490.5 FPts at $5 (Year 3), eligible at both 1B and OF — exactly the positional flexibility LVA needs after losing Soto and Reynolds. He will almost certainly require more than $11 in a competitive auction, but if the budget is enhanced to $18+ via pre-auction releases, Pasquantino becomes a genuine target worth fighting for.

From the list of players other teams may cut before the auction, keep a close eye on Jose Berrios (Texas Rangers, $30 at Year 3). The Rangers are four dollars over auction budget — cuts are coming. If Berrios hits the market, a $10-15 starting pitcher of his caliber at that salary tier is exactly the value play LVA's limited budget should chase. Similarly, Pete Fairbanks (Seattle, $20 at Year 3) is at risk given Seattle's budget constraints. A closer of Fairbanks' track record landing in the auction would directly address the Iglesias hole.

The honest strategic assessment: DiMauro should drop Musgrove immediately and release Herrera before March 22, raising the auction budget to $18. With $18, he can compete seriously for one mid-tier target in the $12-16 range or two smaller acquisitions at $8-10 each. Staying at $11 means watching the entire auction from a distance and hoping the waiver wire and season FAAB can fill the gaps that were created by the offseason cuts. That is not a strategy — it is wishful thinking.

The Draft

MiLB Draft Outlook

LVA enters the 2026 MiLB slow draft (March 15-22) with waiver priority #16 and one open minor league slot. That single slot, combined with the critical call-up window before March 15, shapes the entire draft strategy.

The most important decision DiMauro will make before the draft isn't about who to select — it's whether to call up Max Clark. If Clark is moved to the active roster before the March 15 deadline, two MiLB slots open simultaneously (Clark's slot plus any player moved to accommodate him on the 25-man roster). Two picks at position 16 in a draft cycle, with the flexibility to target two different prospect profiles, is meaningfully better than one pick at the end of the order.

With pick #16 in each round, the premium prospects will be gone. After fifteen teams have selected, the available pool skews toward players with high-upside/high-risk profiles — volatile evaluations, injury histories, or raw toolsets that haven't translated yet. These are exactly the kinds of prospects that fit a system already loaded with near-term contributors like Rodriguez and Celesten. The target profile: a 19-20 year old arm with a high ceiling and development runway, or a middle infield bat that could eventually address the Torres vacancy at 2B.

The existing farm depth in outfield (Clark, Rodriguez), with Celesten providing shortstop coverage, makes adding another outfielder the lowest priority. One premium pitching prospect and one premium position player — ideally with middle infield eligibility — would round out a system that already grades out well for a championship-contending team.

2026 Forecast

Season Prediction

Projected Record: 13–15 wins, 4–6 losses

Most Likely Playoff Seed: #2 or #3

Ceiling: Second TSDL championship. The Judge-Carroll-Lindor-Henderson core is elite. The pitching staff is above average even in a best-case deGrom/Rodon health scenario. In a two-week playoff format, the team that gets the most out of its top performers wins — and LVA has enough top performers to beat anyone.

Floor: 10–9, borderline playoff seed. If deGrom (30 starts in 2025) or Rodon miss significant time and the $11 auction budget leaves no mechanism to patch the rotation, this team is vulnerable in ways that didn't exist when Soto and Iglesias were on the roster. The downside scenario is real, and it starts with a pitcher landing on the IL in April.

The Bold Take: Max Clark gets called up before March 15 and posts 400+ FPts as a free active-roster asset, effectively functioning as a budget auction acquisition that no other team can match. The farm supplements what the auction can't buy. Las Vegas wins another 14-15 regular season games, the pitchers stay healthy enough, and DiMauro claims his second title in three seasons — proving that releasing Juan Soto was either genius or irrelevant because the core was always going to be good enough.

The question isn't whether LVA can compete without Soto. They can. The question is whether they can compete without Soto and without the ability to buy replacements for the inevitable injury or decline. Eleven dollars is the fault line in an otherwise extraordinary roster. Two seasons of dominant baseball have earned DiMauro more than enough benefit of the doubt. The Las Vegas Athletics are still the team everyone else is trying to beat.
