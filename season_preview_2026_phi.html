2026 TSDL Season Preview: Philadelphia Phillies

By Buster Olney — 2026-02-20 — 12 min read

The Philadelphia Phillies have spent four seasons in the Sandlot Dynasty League, and their arc is one of the stranger stories in league history. In 2022 and 2023, they missed the Championship Bracket entirely — finishing 10th and 12th respectively, sent to the consolation round both times while the league's real playoff teams competed for a title without them. Then came 2024, the breakout year: 15-4, the best regular-season record in league history, a unanimous #1 seed, 6,748.5 fantasy points, five individual and team awards. PHI finally made the Championship Bracket — and lost in the first round. One genuine playoff appearance in four seasons. Zero championships.

Ryan Schneider's response to that failure was as deliberate as anything we've seen in this league. He didn't make a quiet adjustment here or trim one expensive contract there and tell himself another run would come. He dismantled the entire thing. In 2025, Schneider traded away Shohei Ohtani ($94 salary), Bryce Harper ($34), Bobby Witt Jr. ($38), Luis Castillo ($17), Pablo Lopez ($16), Blake Snell ($12), and Tyler Glasnow ($10) — a combined $221 in salary contracts, shipped out the door in exchange for prospects, young MLB talent, and future flexibility. The team went 6-13. They finished 16th of 18. They spent October in the consolation bracket.

What Schneider got in return was a minor league system stuffed with top-30 caliber prospects, a core of young MLB contributors locked up on Year 1 and Year 2 deals, and — entering 2026 — the largest free agent auction budget in the entire league. The strip-mining of 2025 wasn't an accident. It was a plan. And if it works, the Phillies aren't just back in the playoffs in 2026 — they're a legitimate championship contender.

Off-Season & Trades

The Art of the Deal — 2025 In-Season Activity

Before a pitch was thrown in 2025, Schneider was already reshaping the Phillies' future through the spring MiLB Draft. Holding the 12th pick in each round, PHI added six new prospects to the organization: Jesus Made (Round 1, pick 12) — a consensus top-5 prospect nationwide; Chandler Simpson (Round 2); Alan Roden (Round 3); Sal Stewart (Round 4); Blade Tidwell (Round 5); and Eduardo Tait (Round 6). Already on the minor league roster and retained without using picks: Matt Shaw, Jacob Wilson, James Triantos, and Kyle Teel — a pre-existing depth class that would soon become trade currency.

As for the 2025 FA Auction: PHI spent exactly $7. Jeff McNeil ($2), Jose Alvarado ($1), Alejandro Kirk ($1), Tylor Megill ($1), Miguel Vargas ($1), and Logan Allen ($1) were the entire haul. That wasn't hoarding — it was a consequence of carrying Ohtani at $94 and Harper at $34. There was no money to spend, and Schneider knew it. The auction window was always going to be 2026. Everything in 2025 was groundwork.

What followed over the next four months was one of the most active trade stretches any TSDL team has engineered in a single season. Fourteen transactions, a relentless pace of acquisition and salary shedding that reshaped not just this roster but the long-term landscape of the league.

The pivot happened in two steps in early April. On April 6, Schneider acquired Shohei Ohtani ($94) from the Colorado Rockies — along with Pablo Lopez, Ricky Tiedemann, and Cris Rodriguez — sending back a collection of MiLB depth pieces (Wilson, Simpson, Shaw, Teel) and spare MLB parts. Ten days later, Ohtani became the most expensive currency in TSDL history when Schneider used him — alongside Brandon Nimmo ($10) and Luis Castillo ($17) — to extract a haul from Atlanta: James Wood ($1, Year 1), Jack Leiter ($1, Year 1), Max Meyer ($2, Year 2), and Reese Olson. In 48 hours, PHI had shed $121 in salary and collected three of the most intriguing young arms and bats available anywhere in the league. Ohtani served as a battering ram against legacy contracts, wielded once and discarded.

The farm system reconstruction arrived in full force on June 25, when a blockbuster with Atlanta sent Pablo Lopez, Blake Snell, Bryce Harper, Bobby Witt Jr., Maikel Garcia, and several MiLB pieces to the Braves in exchange for Jacob Misiorowski, Josue De Paula, Marcelo Mayer, Travis Sykora, and supporting MLB filler. Another $123 in salary obligations — gone. Another four elite minor league prospects — landed.

The supporting moves filled in texture. Brooks Lee arrived from Boston on June 25 (for Kyle Harrison), providing versatile infield depth. Justin Crawford came from Houston on June 28 (for Jorge Polanco and Jack Flaherty). At the August 1 deadline, PHI acquired Thomas White, Kerry Carpenter, and Ryan Jeffers from Las Vegas; Matt Wallner and Trey Yesavage from Miami. Not all of these players stuck — several were themselves cut this offseason — but the accumulation instinct was correct. You grab what you can in a rebuild and sort it out later.

Two waiver-wire pickups deserve separate mention. Emmet Sheehan, claimed June 21, posted 13.6 fantasy points per game across 15 starts after joining the club. Parker Messick, claimed mid-season at $7, averaged an eye-popping 14.6 FPts/G in 7 outings. Both were locked up on $1 Year 1 contracts — entering 2026 at $2 each. That's two potentially elite starting pitchers acquired off the scrap heap, for a combined $2 in 2026 salary.

In-Season Strategy Grade: A — Schneider executed a master class in salary management, prospect accumulation, and future positioning while navigating a rebuild year with minimal drama. The only critique is that some depth pickups at the deadline (Carpenter, Jeffers, Wallner) were immediately cut, suggesting the August acquisitions were more exploratory than deliberate. Minor quibble on an otherwise exceptional body of work.

Season Analysis

2025 Season in Review

The numbers were brutal on the surface. Six wins, thirteen losses. Sixteenth of eighteen teams. A total of 4,856 fantasy points — nearly 1,900 fewer than the 2024 output. The Phillies missed the Championship Bracket entirely, spending the postseason in the consolation round before finishing 17th overall. None of this was accidental.

The weekly progression tells the story with clinical clarity. PHI opened 2-0, briefly touched a #2 power ranking through Week 2, then drifted steadily downward — from 7th to 9th to 12th to 16th — as the roster's best contributors were shipped out and the team thinned to its young core. By mid-summer, the lineup was a mix of cheap keepers and replacement-level pickups. The season low came in Week 17: 164.5 points against San Diego. By then, Schneider had already achieved everything he set out to accomplish. The record was beside the point.

The one constant bright spot was Julio Rodriguez. The franchise cornerstone posted 544 fantasy points across 160 games — the highest total on the roster and a figure that ranked him among the top 20 producers league-wide in 2025. Without J-Rod, this season would have been significantly uglier. James Wood, arriving via trade in April, immediately validated the cost of acquiring him: 465.5 FPts in 157 games, one of the best Year 1 contract performances in the league.

The pitching is where the collapse really lived. PHI's arms generated just 2,436 fantasy points against a league average of 3,099 — a 663-point gap that explains most of the team's losses. The 2025 rotation was assembled on the fly through trades and waiver claims, and while the per-game production from Leiter (10.86 FPts/G), Sheehan (13.6 FPts/G), and Messick (14.6 FPts/G) was genuinely encouraging, the inning volume simply wasn't there. Most critically, Cole Ragans — the only established ace on the staff — was limited to 13 starts by a left shoulder rotator cuff strain that sent him to the 60-day IL and erased what could have been a 350–400 point season.

Hitting FPts: 3,620.5 (League Avg: 3,584.5) — near-average, carried the team
Pitching FPts: 2,436.0 (League Avg: 3,098.9) — 663 pts below league average

The hitting held up. The pitching collapsed. And the closest game of the season — a 3.5-point loss to Chicago in Week 9 — encapsulates how thin the margins were even when PHI was competitive. On a better-constructed 2025 roster, several of those single-digit losses flip. But Schneider wasn't building for 2025. He was building for now.

Schneider didn't lose the 2025 season. He traded it — and got back something far more valuable.
Roster Moves

Offseason Roster Cuts

When the final whistle blew on 2025, Schneider kept cutting. Eleven players were released from the 2025 roster heading into 2026 — a purge that completed the salary reset and locked in the team's auction budget at $199. Most of the cuts were coldly rational. One was unavoidable.

Camilo Doval underwent Tommy John surgery near the end of the 2025 season, eliminating any contribution in 2026 and likely extending his absence into 2027. Doval was on track to be an elite reliever — 277 FPts, 4.0 FPts/G as a Year 1 closer — before the elbow gave out. At a $2 keeper cost, there's a case for holding him as a long-term stash, but a player who cannot pitch for two seasons provides zero fantasy value regardless of eventual upside. He was cut. The right call, made without sentiment.

From there, the logic followed a consistent thread. Josh Jung ($10, Year 4, 273.5 FPts) and Kerry Carpenter ($10, Year 4, 316 FPts) both produced adequately but represent the exact category of player a team rebuilding for the long term should offload: entering Year 4 at non-trivial salary, below-average production relative to their positions, and no meaningful dynasty ceiling. Both cut. Ryan Jeffers ($3, Year 2, 267.5 FPts) and Sean Murphy ($3, Year 2, 188.5 FPts) — both catchers — were released cleanly, a deliberate decision to leave the catching position vacant and address it with the auction budget instead.

Matt Wallner ($5, Year 3, 245 FPts) and Joey Cantillo ($2, Year 2, 222.5 FPts in 34 games) were depth pieces the team no longer needed. Jack Perkins ($2, 108 FPts in 12 games), Sean Murphy already noted, C.J. Kayfus ($2, 88 FPts), and Johan Oviedo ($2, 83 FPts) rounded out the list — all Year 1 contracts that didn't earn a renewal.

The most notable cut on pure production grounds is Ernie Clement. He posted 357.5 FPts across 157 games — the third-highest total on the roster — at a $1 contract that would have cost just $2 to renew. But Clement's profile is a utility infielder with no clear dynasty upside, and Schneider is clearly not building a team around players like him. The decision prioritizes long-term ceiling over short-term efficiency. You can disagree with it — 357 FPts at $2 is exceptional value — but you can understand the logic.

In total, the cuts freed PHI from $43 in keeper salary obligations, landing the roster at $61 total and opening a $199 auction window that is, in a word, transformative.

The Roster

The Core — Returning Players

Twelve confirmed MLB keepers. Total salary: $61. Seven of those twelve enter 2026 at $2 — a collection of Year 2 contracts sitting at the absolute floor of dynasty affordability. Only Julio Rodriguez ($22) and Cole Ragans ($10) carry meaningful salary commitments. The roster chart tells the whole story in a single glance: a team structured to spend, not survive.

Julio Rodriguez

J-Rod is the franchise cornerstone and the most expensive item on this roster — by a wide margin. His 544 FPts across 160 games in 2025 placed him among the top 20 producers in the entire league, and he enters what should be the peak years of his career. At Year 5, the salary escalation is real: he'll cost $27 in 2027, $32 in 2028. The math eventually gets uncomfortable. But right now, $22 for an outfielder who plays every day and produces at an above-average clip is exactly the kind of anchor a contending roster needs. He is the one non-negotiable keeper on this team.

James Wood
OF (eligible: OF) | 2026 Keeper Cost: $2 | Contract Year 2

James Wood is one of the most undervalued contracts in the league. At $2 for a player who posted 465.5 FPts across 157 games as a Year 1 rookie — production that exceeded some of the league's most expensive veterans — Wood represents the best return on investment in the Phillies' rebuild. He'll cost $5 in Year 3, $10 in Year 4. Lock him in now and enjoy two or three more seasons of premium outfield production at near-zero salary. The trade that brought him here — effectively using Shohei Ohtani as currency — is the defining transaction of the Schneider era.

Jack Leiter
SP (eligible: SP) | 2026 Keeper Cost: $2 | Contract Year 2

Jack Leiter averaged 10.86 fantasy points per game across 29 starts in 2025. That is not depth-starter production — that is a number that stands comparison with the better rotation arms in the league, in meaningful if limited volume. At $2 heading into Year 2, Leiter is an automatic hold. The development question isn't whether he becomes a rotation centerpiece; it's how quickly. If he logs 30+ starts in 2026 at anywhere near his 2025 rate, this $2 contract will look like larceny by July.

Emmet Sheehan
SP (eligible: SP) | 2026 Keeper Cost: $2 | Contract Year 2

Claimed off waivers on June 21 for a song, Emmet Sheehan immediately became one of the most productive pickups of the 2025 season — in any league. His 13.6 FPts/G across 15 starts puts him in elite per-game company, and his dynasty profile is among the strongest on this entire roster. At $2 for Year 2, keeping him wasn't a decision; it was automatic. If Sheehan stays healthy and the 2025 flash proves sustainable over a full season, he could emerge as the ace this rotation needs. The ceiling is legitimately significant.

Behind the four headliners, Cole Ragans ($10, Year 4) is the most interesting case on the roster. He missed most of 2025 with a left shoulder rotator cuff strain — limited to 13 starts — but the 10.69 FPts/G in those outings showed exactly what a healthy Ragans produces. His prior-season injury is in the books and, by all indications, resolved. At $10, he's the largest bet PHI is making on a single pitcher, and it's a fair wager for a recovered starter with his track record. If the shoulder flares again, that's a $10 dead end. The hope — and the expectation — is that he opens 2026 fully healthy and takes a full rotation slot.

Jonathan Aranda ($5, Year 3, 289.5 FPts in 106 games) provides a reliable everyday option at first base — his 2.73 FPts/G represents solid middle-lineup production. Parker Messick ($2, Year 2) flashed a remarkable 14.6 FPts/G across 7 outings — the best per-game rate on the entire pitching staff — though a seven-start sample demands appropriate skepticism before anointing him a frontline arm. Max Meyer ($5, Year 3) showed 9.25 FPts/G across 12 starts; the talent is real, the innings questions remain. Ian Seymour ($2, Year 2) contributes as a reliable hybrid SP/RP option at above-average efficiency per outing.

The glaring hole is catching. Both Ryan Jeffers and Sean Murphy were cut this offseason, leaving PHI with zero catchers among their confirmed 2026 keepers. That's a first-order priority at the FA auction — and with $199 available, Schneider has all the firepower he needs to address it aggressively.

Total 2026 auction budget: $199 — the largest in the league by $14 over the next closest team. This is not a coincidence. Every roster move since April 2025 was made with this number in mind.

The Minors

Farm Report

Most TSDL teams have a minor league system. Philadelphia has a minor league asset class. All ten slots are filled, the top of the system is genuinely elite, and — critically — this depth wasn't assembled through draft luck. It was built through deliberate trade work during a season-long rebuild. Only two of the ten current minor leaguers (Jesus Made and Sal Stewart) arrived via the 2025 draft. The rest are the spoils of the salary purge.

Jacob Misiorowski (23) is the system's most advanced piece and the most likely to contribute in 2026. He's already logged 15 MLB appearances, posting 153.5 FPts — strong enough production to earn a rotation conversation. He doesn't appear on prospect lists anymore because he's graduated past that stage: this is an arm with legitimate MLB swing-and-miss stuff who could absorb a full-time role the moment he's activated to the 26-man roster.

Jesus Made (18) is the crown jewel. Drafted 12th overall in the 2025 MiLB Draft, he is a consensus top-5 prospect in this league by any measure — one scouting system places him at #6, another at #3. At 18 years old with off-the-charts offensive projection, Made is a multi-year developmental hold, the kind of asset that becomes a foundational piece of a dynasty when he finally arrives. PHI drafted him and, unlike most of the 2025 draft class, didn't trade him. That restraint was deliberate.

Josue De Paula (20) checks in as a consensus top-16 prospect — a young outfielder with significant offensive upside and years of development ahead. Andrew Painter (22) is a consensus top-20 arm who, when healthy, profiles as a frontline starting pitcher. Injury history has tempered the timeline, but the tools remain elite. Both are multi-year stashes with legitimate ceiling outcomes.

Thomas White (21) grades as a consensus top-27 prospect — a young left-handed starting pitcher with significant projection remaining. Marcelo Mayer (23, consensus top-26) is an elite shortstop prospect who has already seen MLB action (66.5 FPts in 44 games) and is knocking seriously on the door of a full-time job. Sal Stewart (22, consensus top-24) is a third baseman with genuine power upside, acquired via the fourth round of last year's draft. Trey Yesavage (22, consensus top-18) is a pitching arm who had a brief 2025 MLB taste and could push for rotation time in 2026.

Justin Crawford (22, consensus top-39) is a high-upside outfielder from the Houston trade — elite athleticism and speed profile, still developing the offensive tools. Travis Sykora (21) rounds out the system as the deepest piece, a longer-term developmental arm with projection but not near-term impact.

Potential 2026 Call-Up Timeline:

  • Pre-Auction / Opening Day (March 15 deadline): Jacob Misiorowski (MLB-ready, could open on the active roster Day 1); Sal Stewart (one of the most popular sleeper picks in all of fantasy baseball — if he wins the Reds' third base job in spring training, he earns a call-up before the deadline); Trey Yesavage (had 2025 MLB exposure and could lock down a rotation spot in spring); Andrew Painter (when healthy he profiles as a frontline arm — a strong camp could fast-track him); Marcelo Mayer (23, with MLB games already on his ledger and elite shortstop tools, could break camp with his organization)
  • April–June: Justin Crawford, Thomas White (dependent on service-time decisions and MLB organizational needs)
  • Long-Term Stashes (2027+): Jesus Made, Josue De Paula, Travis Sykora

Schneider faces a legitimately interesting problem heading into March: up to five minor leaguers — Misiorowski, Stewart, Yesavage, Painter, and Mayer — each have a plausible path to meaningful MLB roles before Opening Day. The likelihood that all five break camp in significant roles is essentially zero; spring training will be the filter. But the talent concentration at the top of this system is real, and every player PHI promotes before March 15 opens a MiLB slot in the draft. Spring training evaluation matters more for this organization than almost any other in the league.

This is not a rebuilding farm system. This is a fully-assembled one — and it's about to start paying dividends.

Farm System Grade: A− The system lacks organizational depth at catcher and the lower levels beyond Made are unproven. But from top to bottom, this is one of the most prospect-rich organizations in the league — with five consensus top-30 prospects and another three knocking on that door. The grade would be a flat A with one more premium catcher in the pipeline.

Bubble Watch

Drop Watch — Pre-Auction Speculation

PHI's offseason cuts were sweeping — eleven players gone. Most of the easy decisions have already been made. But a few players on the confirmed 2026 keeper list remain worth scrutinizing before the March 22 auction deadline.

Brooks Lee (eligible: 2B, 3B, SS — $5, Year 3) — Lee posted 288 FPts across 139 games in 2025, which sounds passable until you work out the rate: 2.07 FPts/G. That is replacement-level production from a player occupying premium infield positions and costing $5 heading into Year 3. The case to cut is real. If Schneider lands Austin Riley, Matt Chapman, or Dansby Swanson at the auction — all legitimate targets given the budget — Lee becomes redundant at best and a roster-construction problem at worst. His dynasty profile is thin enough that sentiment shouldn't keep him around. A $5 slot freed now is worth more than 2.1 FPts/G all season.

Dylan Beavers (eligible: OF — $2, Year 2) — Beavers posted 84 FPts in 35 games (2.4 FPts/G) in a limited 2025 audition. At $2, the risk of holding is minimal, and his dynasty profile is respectable enough to justify the cheap stash. This is a monitor-not-cut situation today. But if PHI goes heavy on outfield acquisitions at the auction, Beavers becomes a roster-crunch casualty. Worth keeping through spring; worth reassessing if the outfield picture changes after the auction.

Carson Benge (eligible: 1B, OF — $2, Year 2) — No 2025 statistics on record. Benge has genuine dynasty value and enough youth to project multiple development paths, so at $2, holding him costs almost nothing. But a player with zero production history in the database is always a question mark when roster flexibility is tight. Hold him through spring — and if he's not contributing at the MLB level by June 1, have the conversation. A $2 dead slot is still a slot.

Notably absent from this list: Cole Ragans. Yes, $10 is real money, and yes, the 2025 shoulder injury raises legitimate questions. But a healthy Ragans is worth 350-plus fantasy points. Cutting him for $10 in savings while he's on track to recover would be exactly the wrong move — trading away a potentially elite arm at the precise moment his price is most reasonable in the contract cycle.

Auction Strategy

Free Agent Auction Strategy

One hundred and ninety-nine dollars. Let that number sit for a moment. The Philadelphia Phillies enter the 2026 FA Auction with $199 in available budget — the confirmed figure, not a projection. The next closest team is Toronto at $185. Then Chicago Cubs at $167. The gap between PHI and the league's median spender isn't a slight advantage; it's the difference between competing for elite free agents and watching them go to someone else.

Teams with budget dominate the auction. It is one of the few iron laws of TSDL. San Diego, Seattle, and Houston — three of the league's strongest 2025 teams — are entering this auction with negative budgets. They will be bidding minimally, if at all, for the marquee acquisitions. PHI will be bidding on everything it wants. That is a structural advantage that cannot be overstated, and it is precisely what two years of salary purging was designed to create.

Here is what PHI objectively needs: a catcher (zero keepers at the position — this is a top priority), proven infield depth behind Jonathan Aranda (Jung and Lee departures leave the lineup thin at third base and shortstop), and established starting pitching depth to insulate against the health risk in a rotation full of young arms with limited innings history. The confirmed free agent pool has answers to all three. Here is how to spend.

Priority Target: Shohei Ohtani (OF/SP) — Ohtani headlines the 2026 free agent pool, and the Phillies know exactly what he brings to a roster — they've had him before. PHI has a history with the most valuable player in fantasy baseball, and bringing him back for a third season would complete a remarkable full-circle moment for a franchise that briefly held and traded him at the peak of their 2025 rebuild. No other team in the league is better positioned to win this bidding war: with $199 available, PHI can outspend every competitor in the room for the consensus best player on the board. The calculus is simple — Ohtani as a confirmed keeper at a Year 1 cost transforms this roster from a promising rebuild into a title contender overnight. Suggested range: $80–$100. The cost will be steep, but PHI built this budget precisely for a moment like this.

If the Ohtani bidding exceeds what PHI wants to pay long-term, the pitching alternatives at the top of the free agent pool are Kevin Gausman and Jack Flaherty. Gausman is an established frontline starter with elite strikeout potential; Flaherty is a name PHI knows well — he passed through their hands in the 2025 Houston trade, and his effectiveness in a full, healthy season makes him a legitimate rotation anchor. Either significantly upgrades the rotation's ceiling at a fraction of the Ohtani price. Suggested range for either: $20–$35.

Priority Target: Edwin Diaz (RP) — Without Doval's Tommy John wiping out the closer situation, PHI enters 2026 without a proven late-inning anchor. Edwin Diaz is one of the best available relievers in the confirmed pool — a high-leverage weapon with a legitimate track record in dominant closing situations. At the right price, he fills an immediate and significant gap. Suggested range: $12–$20.

Third Base — Riley, Chapman, or Muncy — Josh Jung is gone and the 3B position is vacant. PHI should target at least one legitimate everyday third baseman at the auction. The names to watch: Austin Riley, who profiles as one of the better power bats in the free agent class; Matt Chapman, an elite defender with consistent offensive production and multi-year dynasty value; and Max Muncy, a confirmed free agent who provides 3B/1B flexibility at a reasonable price point. Any of these three meaningfully upgrades what is currently a hole in the lineup. Suggested ranges: Riley and Chapman in the $20–$32 band; Muncy as the budget-conscious option at $10–$16. And whichever 3B Schneider lands almost certainly makes the Brooks Lee decision easier — cut the fringe infielder and slot in an actual starter.

Shortstop — Betts or Swanson — With Brooks Lee as the primary SS option, the middle infield is thin. The free agent market may offer an upgrade worth pursuing. Mookie Betts — if available — is the kind of versatile star who changes a roster's identity immediately; his ability to contribute at multiple positions makes him a perfect fit for a team that needs infield flexibility alongside outfield depth. Dansby Swanson is a more straightforward shortstop option — an everyday player with solid production and a track record of durability. Either provides a genuine starting SS rather than a below-average placeholder. Suggested range: Betts at $30–$45 given his versatility and star profile; Swanson at $15–$24. Landing either of these alongside an Ohtani or pitching acquisition would give PHI one of the most complete rosters in the league.

For catching — the most pressing need — the free agent pool is actually quite rich at the position, and PHI is perfectly situated to capitalize. Will Smith is the headliner: an elite, everyday catcher with genuine star production who rarely hits the market. If Smith is available, PHI should be the most aggressive bidder in the room — he is the kind of foundational backstop that anchors a roster for years. Suggested range: $25–$40. Beyond Smith, the depth at catcher is legitimate: JT Realmuto brings a proven all-around profile; Yainer Diaz is a younger, high-upside option at a likely lower price point; Logan O'Hoppe is one of the more intriguing developmental catchers in the game; Carlos Narvaez and Ryan Jeffers (whom PHI cut this offseason) round out a catching class that offers options at multiple price points. With $199 to spend, PHI can win a bidding war for Will Smith and still have budget remaining for the rest of their needs. There is no excuse for entering 2026 without a legitimate catcher.

The secondary market also warrants attention. Vladimir Guerrero Jr. (if Miami releases him from a $73 contract) is the kind of superstar that PHI's budget can uniquely absorb. Trea Turner (if Cincinnati moves on from his $48 salary) would provide elite shortstop production. Neither is guaranteed to hit the market — but if either does, PHI is the one team in the league positioned to win that bidding war without blinking.

One underrated strategy worth considering: use a slice of the budget to acquire a marquee player with the explicit intention of trading him to a salary-constrained team in exchange for prospects or controlled-cost assets. The ideal candidate here is Juan Soto. PHI already fields a deep, productive outfield with Julio Rodriguez and James Wood as the anchors — adding Soto would create an outfield glut they can't realistically sustain long-term at his salary escalation. But winning Soto at auction and immediately shopping him to a team that couldn't afford to win that bid? That's leverage. PHI could walk away with a premium prospect haul or a salary-favorable star at a position of need. With $199 available, Schneider is uniquely positioned to execute a move no other team in this league can pull off.

Final point: unused auction budget does not carry over. Every dollar left on the table at auction close is a wasted dollar. With $199, there is no excuse for leaving more than $10–$15 unspent. This is the payoff for everything PHI sacrificed in 2025. The window is now. Spend accordingly.

The Draft

MiLB Draft Outlook

PHI enters the 2026 MiLB slow draft (March 15–22) with all ten minor league slots occupied — meaning every new pick requires a promotion first. The good news is that spring training may naturally create those openings. As discussed in the Farm Report, up to five prospects carry legitimate paths to the active MLB roster before the March 15 deadline: Misiorowski, Stewart, Yesavage, Painter, and Mayer. If even two or three of them earn spots, PHI enters the draft with real flexibility to add new talent at the bottom of the system.

PHI holds the 9th pick in each draft round — mid-field positioning with reasonable board access. Given the system is already stacked at starting pitcher and outfield, Schneider should target positional need over raw upside in the draft. The farm is thin at catcher — precisely mirroring the vacancy on the MLB active roster. Target athletic young backstops in the first two rounds; complement with right-handed power at corner infield in the middle rounds. The system does not need another projection arm. It needs a backstop pipeline to develop alongside this talented core, and the draft is where that starts.

The Verdict

2026 Season Prediction

Projected Record: 10–9 to 12–7

Projected Finish: 4th to 6th overall

Playoff Seed: 4–6 (Championship Bracket)

Ceiling: Top-3 seed, legitimate championship contender — if Ragans logs 30 healthy starts, Leiter and Sheehan break out over a full season, and the auction budget is deployed with precision

Floor: 8th seed, first-round playoff exit — if pitching health becomes an issue again and the auction additions fail to compensate

The 2026 season hinges on three questions for Ryan Schneider: Does Cole Ragans stay on the mound for 30 starts? Do Jack Leiter and Emmet Sheehan prove that their jaw-dropping per-game numbers hold up over a full season of work? And does the $199 auction budget get deployed wisely enough to fill the catching void and shore up the infield? If the answers are yes, yes, and yes — this team is a dark horse for the title. If the pitching health card gets played again, they're looking at a 9-10 win season, another first-round exit, and a third trip to the rebuild drawing board. The farm system provides long-term cushion regardless of what happens this year. But after two years of sacrifice, 2026 is when the Phillies need to prove the investment was worth it.

Four seasons. One Championship Bracket appearance. Zero championships. In 2024, Schneider had the most dominant regular-season machine in franchise history — and walked away from the postseason empty-handed after just one round. Rather than run it back, he chose the harder path: tear it down, rebuild from within, and trust that the foundation would hold.

The foundation held. Julio Rodriguez and James Wood anchor what could become one of the better outfield duos in the league. Jack Leiter, Emmet Sheehan, Parker Messick, Cole Ragans, and Max Meyer form a rotation with legitimate ace upside if they stay healthy and build on their 2025 production rates. The farm system — Jesus Made, Josue De Paula, Andrew Painter, Marcelo Mayer, and the rest — is among the deepest and most talented in the league. And $199 in auction budget means Schneider can put the finishing touches on a roster that is, finally, built not just to compete but to win.

The only thing standing between Ryan Schneider and a championship in 2026 is execution. He's had the patience to rebuild. He's had the vision to stockpile. Now comes the hardest part: spending $199 wisely, keeping his pitchers healthy, and closing out a playoff run that four years of work has been building toward. After everything 2025 cost — the wins, the fun, the superstar lineup — this time, it better.
