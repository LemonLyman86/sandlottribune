Los Angeles Dodgers Make First Moves In TSDL

Jacob's new franchise announces its four keeper picks — and the decision to pass on Kristian Campbell says more about this ownership group than any press release ever could.

By Jason Stark (@jaysonst) — March 8, 2026 — 7 min read

The new Los Angeles Dodgers have made their first official declaration of organizational identity, and it turns out it says as much about what they didn't keep as what they did.

Jacob — the California-based owner who purchased the Texas Rangers, renamed them the Dodgers, and has declined to share his surname with this publication — submitted his keeper list on Saturday, locking in four players from the inherited Texas roster. The choices are: Cristopher Sanchez, SP, Philadelphia Phillies; Cade Smith, RP, Cleveland Guardians; Hunter Goodman, C, Colorado Rockies; and Bryce Rainer, SP prospect, Detroit Tigers organization.

Combined salary: $27. Combined contract years after reset: four players at Year 1. It is a foundation, not a roster — but that, it would appear, is entirely the point.

The Decision

Four Keepers, One Clear Vision

Let's put numbers on the board, because the numbers are genuinely interesting.

Cristopher Sanchez is the easy call, so let's not spend too many words on it. The Phillies left-hander is 27 years old, coming off consecutive seasons in one of baseball's best rotations, and has established himself as exactly the kind of structurally sound starter — elite ground-ball rate, filthy changeup, improving command on both sides — that wins fantasy leagues and real ones alike. Keeping him at $20 with the contract clock resetting to Year 1 is, in the clinical language of dynasty analysis, a steal. It is also the most obvious decision Jacob will make this offseason, which does not make it any less correct.

Hunter Goodman at $2 is a positional bet as much as a player bet. Catching is thin in the TSDL pool, and Goodman — despite the disadvantage of calling games for a Rockies staff — has raw power numbers that project as genuine fantasy production at a premium position. At $2 into a Year 1 reset, the downside is exactly two dollars and one roster spot. Jacob has already won this pick.

Bryce Rainer at zero dollars occupies a minor league slot and costs the new owner essentially nothing but optimism. Rainer has the arm that evaluators in the Detroit organization have been quietly talking about for two years. He may be two seasons away from contributing at the major league level. He may be more. But at $0, Jacob gets to find out for free, and in a league where minor league depth is currency, a zero-cost pitching prospect with a legitimate ceiling is always worth the slot.

And then there is Cade Smith.

Smith is the one that requires some explanation. The Cleveland Guardians reliever enters the 2026 season with genuine closer upside — his strikeout rate is elite, his stuff plays in high-leverage situations, and he is only 26 years old. Keeping him at $5 into a Year 1 reset is defensible on its own. The reason his selection is generating conversation is not what it says about Cade Smith. It is what it says about Kristian Campbell.

"Sanchez's reaction when hearing he was one of the four players selected to remain with the Dodgers: 'I'm excited to stay with the Dodgers and contribute to the team's success in the upcoming season. I look forward to working hard and helping the team compete for a championship.'" — Cristopher Sanchez, in a statement released through the club
The Notable Omission

Campbell Goes to the Pool

Kristian Campbell — the Boston Red Sox's second baseman, the player many dynasty analysts had penciled into this keeper class since the sale was announced — is available in the expansion pool.

This is a choice. Jacob had the slot. Campbell's $2 salary and Year 1 reset projection made him, by almost any conventional evaluation model, the most valuable keeper available after Sanchez. He is 23 years old. He was one of the better prospects in baseball eighteen months ago. He is already in the major leagues, hitting in the middle of one of the better lineups in the American League. He is the kind of asset that most expansion owners would protect without deliberating past the first coffee of the morning.

Jacob chose Cade Smith instead. The decision is not indefensible — Smith's closer upside and age make him a legitimate asset at his salary point — but it reflects a philosophical orientation that bears watching. A closer who generates saves is valuable in a points-based format. A second baseman with Campbell's offensive profile is valuable in almost every format, for approximately a decade. The Dodgers may have prioritized a year or two of production over a longer runway of elite performance. Or Jacob has information about Campbell's role in Boston that the rest of us do not. Either way, the pool just got more interesting.

Also entering the pool from the Texas inheritance: Freddie Freeman ($42, 1B, LAD), whose salary made him a non-starter for the keeper decision; Willson Contreras ($13, C, BOS), a veteran at a position of depth; and Harry Ford ($0, C prospect, WSH), a catcher whose floor and ceiling remain in vigorous dispute among scouts.

League Reaction

Not Everyone Asked for This

It is worth pausing here, before we get to the pool analysis, to acknowledge that the mechanism that produced this entire situation — the expansion draft trigger — has not been universally celebrated around the TSDL.

The rule that activates an expansion draft when ownership transfers requires every existing team to expose players they might prefer not to expose. That is, by design, the point. But design and preference do not always coexist comfortably, and the owners who had to submit protection lists spent a portion of the last two weeks making exactly that observation, loudly.

"I'd like to…abolish the rule that can trigger an expansion draft. People need to be a man and just take over a roster."

— Cory Aleci, TSDL owner

Aleci's position is not without logic. There is an argument — a real one — that the cleaner solution to an ownership transfer is a straight assumption of the existing roster, contracts intact, no exposure required. The new owner inherits exactly what the previous owner built, for better or worse, and competes accordingly. No protection lists. No pool. No seventeen front offices spending two weeks in quiet panic deciding which $31 outfielder they can stomach losing.

Not everyone was quite as pointed in their opposition, but the sentiment, once you started listening for it, was widespread.

"I won't bitch…but I definitely don't like it."

— Tim Dacey, TSDL owner

Tim Dacey — who did not bitch, as promised, but did make the record on the matter — speaks for a segment of this ownership group that views the expansion draft as an involuntary tax on well-managed rosters. The rule does not discriminate between teams that built carefully and teams that accumulated assets carelessly. If you protected twenty players and still had someone like Kristian Campbell left exposed, it means you built something good enough to be worth stealing from. That is not nothing. It is also not obviously fair.

Whether the rule survives to the next ownership transition is now, at minimum, a conversation. Jacob's good fortune in arriving to a 189-player pool of extraordinary depth may be part of that conversation. The rule is what it is, for now. But the league has heard from its owners.

The Outlook

A Foundation, Not a Finished Product

The numbers are favorable. An auction budget north of $230, ten open minor league slots, and a pool that contains Juan Soto and Kristian Campbell — among approximately 187 other options — gives the new Dodgers a degree of optionality that most expansion franchises spend years wishing they had. Jacob has, by keeping his keeper salaries low, preserved maximum flexibility for the Free Agent Auction and the expansion picks themselves.

Whether that flexibility gets deployed wisely is the only question that matters now, and it is a question that the next few weeks will answer. In this league, new owners have a tendency to either overreach — spending $95 on a single cornerstone because the ceiling is irresistible — or underreach — assembling a cheap roster that looks diversified and turns out to be mediocre. The Dodgers' keeper decisions suggest a franchise that has thought carefully about controlled costs and long-term contract structure. Now comes the harder part: building a roster around those four players that can actually compete in 2026 while setting up something sustainable for 2027 and beyond.

The choice to keep Cade Smith over Kristian Campbell will be revisited in this column approximately once per month for the next three years. That is not a criticism; it is the nature of dynasty decisions. You make your picks and you live with them in the standings, and the standings eventually tell you whether you were right. If Smith closes 35 games and Dodger Stadium vibrates with saves while the owner who took Campbell from the pool celebrates a playoff berth, the column will write itself. If Smith stumbles and Campbell becomes an All-Star, same thing.

Either way, the Dodgers are in business. The expansion pool is open. The league is watching.

Four players. Twenty-seven dollars. Let's see what Jacob builds.
